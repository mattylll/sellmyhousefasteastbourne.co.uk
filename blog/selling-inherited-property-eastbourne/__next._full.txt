11:T1e2b,<h2>Inheriting a Property in Eastbourne</h2><p>Eastbourne's older demographic means that inherited properties are a significant feature of the local property market. The town's popularity with retirees over many decades has created a substantial stock of homes whose owners eventually pass away, leaving their properties to be managed by executors and distributed to beneficiaries. If you are in this position, this guide covers everything you need to know about selling an inherited property in the Eastbourne area.</p><h2>The Probate Process</h2><h3>What Is Probate?</h3><p>Probate is the legal process of administering a deceased person's estate. When someone dies owning property in Eastbourne, the executor named in their will (or an administrator appointed by the court if there is no will) is responsible for managing the estate. Before the inherited property can be sold, the executor typically needs to obtain a Grant of Probate from the Probate Registry, which confirms their legal authority to deal with the estate's assets.</p><h3>Can You Sell Before Probate?</h3><p>You cannot complete the sale until probate has been granted, but you can begin marketing, accept offers, and instruct solicitors while waiting. Cash buyers are well placed to work around probate timelines because they do not have mortgage deadlines or chains creating pressure to complete by a specific date. We regularly purchase probate properties in Eastbourne and can structure the transaction around whatever timeline the probate process requires.</p><h2>Costs of Holding an Inherited Property in Eastbourne</h2><p>The ongoing costs of holding an empty inherited property in Eastbourne can be substantial. Council tax applies to empty properties, and Eastbourne Borough Council may charge a premium on properties that have been empty for extended periods. Buildings insurance is essential and costs more for empty properties. Utility bills continue, and maintaining minimum heating is important to prevent damp and frost damage, particularly in period properties.</p><p>Coastal-specific costs add to the burden. Sea air accelerates deterioration of external surfaces, windows, and metalwork. Gutters, downpipes, and roof flashings may need attention. Garden maintenance is necessary to prevent the property looking neglected, which can attract antisocial behaviour or simply depress the eventual sale price. On a typical Eastbourne property, holding costs can amount to £400 to £600 per month — £2,400 to £3,600 over six months.</p><h2>Common Challenges with Inherited Properties in Eastbourne</h2><h3>Period Properties Needing Modernisation</h3><p>Inherited properties in Meads, Old Town, and Upperton are often period buildings that have not been updated for decades. A Victorian or Edwardian house owned by an elderly relative may have dated wiring, an aging boiler, original single-glazed sash windows, a kitchen and bathroom from the 1970s or 1980s, and tired decoration throughout. While the property may be structurally sound, it falls well short of modern expectations.</p><p>The cost of modernising a period property in Eastbourne can be substantial. A full rewire costs £3,500 to £6,000, a new boiler and heating system £3,000 to £5,000, sash window restoration or replacement £1,000 to £2,500 per window, a new kitchen £5,000 to £15,000, and a new bathroom £3,000 to £8,000. Add in decoration, new flooring, and any structural repairs, and the total can easily reach £40,000 to £70,000 for a larger period house.</p><p>Cash buyers purchase in any condition, eliminating the need for renovation. The offer reflects the property's current state, but the savings in time, cost, and stress are significant.</p><h3>Leasehold Complications</h3><p>If the inherited property is a leasehold flat, the remaining lease length is a critical factor. Many of Eastbourne's converted Victorian and Edwardian flats have leases that have been running for decades, and some have fallen below the 80-year threshold where mortgage lending becomes restricted and lease extension costs rise significantly.</p><p>For executors, dealing with a short lease adds complexity and cost to the sale process. A lease extension can cost tens of thousands of pounds and take months to arrange. Cash buyers purchase short-lease properties without requiring the lease to be extended first, providing a simpler route to sale.</p><h3>Retirement Property Restrictions</h3><p>Some inherited properties in Eastbourne may be retirement or sheltered housing with age restrictions. These properties can only be sold to buyers who meet the age criteria, significantly limiting the buyer pool. The specific restrictions, management company requirements, and service charge obligations all need to be understood and communicated to potential buyers.</p><p>Cash buyers experienced in the Eastbourne market understand these restrictions and can purchase retirement properties without difficulty, provided they are able to identify a qualifying onward buyer or hold the property in their portfolio.</p><h3>Properties with Contents</h3><p>Inherited properties in Eastbourne often contain a lifetime of belongings. Clearing a property is time-consuming, emotionally difficult, and can be expensive if professional clearance services are needed. The distance that many beneficiaries live from Eastbourne adds to the practical challenge.</p><p>We buy properties with contents included. There is no need to clear the property before selling to us — we arrange clearance after completion, removing this burden from executors entirely.</p><h2>Tax Considerations</h2><h3>Inheritance Tax</h3><p>Inheritance tax may be payable on the estate, including the value of the inherited property. The current IHT threshold is £325,000 per person, with an additional residence nil-rate band of £175,000 when a home is passed to direct descendants. Given Eastbourne's average property price of £290,000, many inherited properties will fall within or close to these thresholds, though properties in Meads, Old Town, and other premium areas may exceed them.</p><h3>Capital Gains Tax</h3><p>If the inherited property increases in value between the date of death and the date of sale, capital gains tax may be payable on the gain. The property is valued at its probate value (market value at the date of death), and any increase is potentially subject to CGT. A fast cash sale that completes soon after probate minimises the window for price changes and simplifies the tax position.</p><h2>Selling Options</h2><h3>Estate Agent</h3><p>Listing with a local Eastbourne estate agent takes four to six months, potentially longer due to seasonal factors. Costs include agent commission, solicitor fees, and EPC charges. Properties needing work may take longer to sell.</p><h3>Auction</h3><p>Auction provides a defined timeline (typically 28 days to completion) but with uncertain outcome. Auction fees can be 2% to 3%, and there is no guarantee the property will reach its reserve.</p><h3>Cash Buyer</h3><p>Selling to a cash buyer offers the fastest and most certain route. We make a free cash offer within 24 hours, complete in as little as two weeks, and buy in any condition with contents included. No fees, and we cover basic legal costs.</p><h2>Steps for Executors</h2><p>The practical steps are: obtain the death certificate and begin probate. Secure the property and arrange insurance. Obtain valuations from multiple sources. Once probate is granted, accept an offer and instruct solicitors.
