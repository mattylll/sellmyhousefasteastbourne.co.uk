Property Landscape in Old Town

Old Town is the historic heart of Eastbourne, a settlement that existed centuries before the Victorian developers transformed the seafront into the fashionable resort it became. Centred around the ancient church of St Mary the Virgin and the Lamb Inn, which has stood since the 12th century, Old Town has a village atmosphere that is entirely distinct from the formal Victorian character of central Eastbourne.

The housing stock in Old Town reflects this long history. While few truly ancient buildings survive among the residential properties, the area has a rich mix of period architecture. Victorian and Edwardian houses predominate, ranging from modest terraced cottages to more substantial semi-detached and detached homes. Some Georgian properties survive near the High Street, and there are 18th and early 19th century cottages that predate the Victorian expansion. More modern infill developments have added some contemporary housing, but the area's character remains firmly rooted in its historic identity.

Old Town's appeal lies in its combination of period character, independent shops, and community feel. The weekly farmers' market, the independent restaurants and cafes along the High Street, and the proximity to both the town centre and the South Downs create a neighbourhood that attracts a broad demographic, from young professionals to downsizers seeking character and convenience.

Property prices in Old Town are generally above the Eastbourne average, reflecting the area's desirability and the period character of its housing stock. We buy all types of property in Old Town, whether they are beautifully maintained period homes or buildings that need significant restoration work.

Why People Sell in Old Town

Old Town's established residential character and diverse demographic mean that we see a range of reasons for wanting a fast sale. The area's appeal spans generations, and the motivations for selling reflect this breadth.

Inherited properties are particularly common in Old Town. The area's longstanding residential character means many homes have been in families for decades. When a relative passes away, their Old Town property may need work — perhaps the sash windows need refurbishing, the heating system is outdated, or the kitchen and bathroom have not been updated since the 1980s. For executors managing estates, the prospect of a lengthy renovation before marketing is unwelcome. We buy inherited properties in any condition.

Downsizing is another common motivation. Old Town's period houses, while charming, can be impractical for older residents. Steep stairs, large gardens, and the maintenance demands of older buildings all contribute to the desire to move to something more manageable. The speed and simplicity of a cash sale appeals to sellers who want to get on with the next chapter rather than spending months in the uncertainty of a traditional sale.

Relationship breakdowns and financial pressures also bring Old Town homeowners to us. In both cases, the ability to sell quickly and with certainty provides a resolution that months of estate agent marketing cannot match. We handle all these situations with discretion and work to achieve the best possible outcome for the seller.

Local Market Conditions

Old Town occupies a strong position in the Eastbourne property market. The area's period character, village atmosphere, and central-but-distinct location make it perennially popular with buyers, supporting values above the town average. Three-bedroom Victorian and Edwardian houses typically sell in the £320,000 to £420,000 range, while smaller cottages and terraces can be found from £250,000. Larger detached properties command higher figures still.

Demand in Old Town is generally steady, with the area's character attracting buyers who are specifically seeking period homes and a village feel within walking distance of Eastbourne's amenities. However, the seasonal pattern that affects the wider Eastbourne market applies here too — spring and summer bring more active buyers, while the winter months can be quieter.

Period properties needing significant work can face particular challenges in the traditional market. Buyers at Old Town price points often want properties that are ready to move into, and the prospect of major renovation can narrow the field. Conservation area restrictions in parts of Old Town add a further consideration for buyers planning alterations. Cash buyers who purchase in any condition provide a valuable alternative for sellers of properties that would struggle on the open market.

Transport and Amenities

Old Town sits within easy walking distance of Eastbourne town centre, the railway station, and the seafront, giving it excellent accessibility despite its village-like character. The High Street is the focal point of the neighbourhood, with independent shops, cafes, restaurants, and the popular weekly farmers' market creating a vibrant local centre that serves both residents and visitors.

The Lamb Inn, one of the oldest pubs in East Sussex, anchors the historic end of the High Street and is a focal point for community life. The church of St Mary the Virgin, parts of which date back to the 12th century, provides a link to Eastbourne's pre-Victorian past. The Towner Art Gallery, housed in a striking modern building, is a short walk from Old Town and has established itself as one of the leading contemporary art spaces on the south coast.

Transport connections from Old Town include easy access to Eastbourne railway station, which provides regular services to London Victoria, Brighton, and Lewes. Bus services connect Old Town to other parts of Eastbourne and the wider East Sussex area. The A22, which passes close to Old Town, provides road access northward towards the M25 and London. The A259 coast road connects to Brighton and Hastings.

The South Downs rise immediately to the north and west of Old Town, providing exceptional access to the national park for walking, cycling, and riding. The Downs are a defining feature of Eastbourne's identity and a major amenity for residents in Old Town and the surrounding areas. Local schools, both primary and secondary, are within reach, and Eastbourne's broader educational provision includes colleges and adult education centres.
